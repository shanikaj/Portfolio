. Landmarks Around The World
Mar 2020 - Present

This past March drastically changed the day-to-day lives of many - including myself - although I can't say I wholeheartedly miss sprinting to the train platform every day in hopes of discovering an empty seat. Nonetheless, these leftover 12 hours a week have created a window of opportunity!

Inspired by my past travels (and likely lack of travels in the near future), I decided to take a trip down memory lane and model some iconic landmarks across the globe that I have had the privilege of visiting, to further improve my CAD skills. And to make it more challenging, I strayed away from tourist attractions that have already amassed a plethora of models, such as the Eiffel Tower.

I first modelled the Roman Colosseum, intrigued by its current run-down state. To create this structure, elliptically shapped inner and outer walls were sketched primarily through the use of the swept boss/base feature. The windows and interior seating were driven by the curve driven pattern tool. The area floor itself is deteriorated, exposing the hypogeum underneath, consisting of tunnels and cages. Finally, the exterior walls have been "ruined" by extruded cuts to truly replicate the years of damage the amphitheatre has endured.

Next I modelled the Marina Bay Sands in Singapore! The unique design in each of its three towers - including asymmetric, curved legs leaning against the other, involved various extrusions and plenty of 3D sketches. The SkyPark, located at the top of the structure, contains a cantilevered segment and infinity pool. Although I was never a resident of the hotel and only visited the Observation deck, I did make sure to include plenty of lounge chairs!

. Line Following Robot
Sep 2019 - Dec 2019

Previous Next

The lab portion of my Sensors & Instrumentation course involved the constructing the electronics for a small, mobile robot, thereby introducing myself to the practical aspects of sensor implementation and interfacing. Throughout the term, I was exposed to component tolerances, noise, and various design trade-offs.

The basic infrastructure for this robot consisted of the power regulation, microcontroller, and integrated programmer, and variety of sensors. The sensor suite included a push button, optical encoders for measuring the rotation of the wheels, thermistor for temperature measurement, a Hall effect sensor fir measuring the strength of a magnetic field, photo-diodes used for infrared light detection to follow a line on the ground, and overall system current sensing.

Achieving a fully functional robot involved performing wiring and soldering based on electrical schematics and drawings of various components, including op-amps, capacitors, transistors, resonators, LED's, and resistors.

. Balsa Wood Truss
Feb 2019 - Mar 2019

Previous Next

The purpose of this project was to create a 3D truss with optimum strength-weight ratio for my Mechanics of Deformable Solids course using balsa wood as truss members and wooden dowels as joints. The components were drawn in AutoCAD and laser cut for testing purposes. An upright Warren truss bridge with three equilateral triangles structure was used as the basis for constructing and testing, varying in the design of truss members.

One of the designs consisted of I-beams for the compressive members to prevent bending bending of buckling, while the tensile members had one elongated hole along the centre to reduce weight. Unforunately, this bridge failed earlier than expected due to link tear out in every member, as well as twisting of the tensile members. Ultimately, the bridge with the minimum weight that was optimized to carry the maximum load consisted of simple, thin beams for decreased weight. Additionally, washers were glued onto the ends of compressive members at the pin joints to avoid twisting at the dowel.

Guitar Playing Robot
Oct 2017 - Dec 2017

The term project for my Introduction to Mechatronics Engineering course was the design of a robotic system using a Lego EV3 system in conjunction with various sensors. My group decided to create a guitar playing robot, consisting of two main systems corresponding to the functionalities of human hands when playing the instrument: a chord presser and a strummer. To play various songs, the robot was programmed using C++ and RobotC.

The chord component presses down on the strings in accordance to four chords (G major, D major, E minor, and C major), while the strumming component plucks all six strings of the guitar. A frame keeps all the components of the robot together in a stable and secure placement. The strumming mechanism consisted of a 360 degree rotating arm with three evenly spread axles was created with guitar picks attached to the ends.

The final design of the chord presser is more complicated in nature. One of the challenges involved was ensuring that the guitar does not become damaged. This was proven to be quite difficult as we needed to test the amount of pressure that the chord presser needed to apply on the strings in order for the strumming mechanism to produce a sound, musical notes.

Acknowledging this, the chord pressing mechanism involved two series of three axles: one series hovers over the three frets and the other series is parallel, 7cm above. Each axle is separated into six parts, corresponding to the six strings of the guitar. The bottom series of axles consists of exrtusions that press down on the frets only when the top series of axles is aligned such that it is able to press down on said extrusions. The axles are connected to the main axle that is connected to a motor. The motor is programmed to rotate for every multiple of 90 degrees, which corresponds to the four chords.

Cell Phone Stand

Previous Next

As a project for my Engineering Graphics and Design course, I was tasked with designed a 3D printed cell phone stand in SolidWorks. I used a deathly hallows design inspired by Harry Potter for the basis to ensure strength and resiliency through the use of triangles. The phone is held in a stable position, at an angle of 70 degrees.

This exercise allowed me to gain exposure to FDM print orientations and the optimization of the printing process. Additional holes were cut out to save on material, although if I were to do it again, I would remove part of the backend as it wasn't needed in the end for the stand to function successfully. Furthermore, I would have added more intricately designed hole cut-outs to create a more aesthetically pleasing design, now that I have more CAD experience.

. DIY Distance Sensor
Sep 2018 - Dec 2018 | Richmond Hill, ON

For my Introduction to Statistical Analysis course, my classmates and I were tasked with the design of a distance measurement system to measure the thickness of a Canadian coin. This measurement system was built on first principles through the use of a potentiometer as part of a team of 4.

The system consisted of a clamp and a 3D printed mechanism. The movement of the top of the clamp upwards allows the larger gear to rotate, consequently rotating the smaller gear. This gear turns to a simple potentiometer, converting the gear rotations (which correlate to coin thickness) to a measureable voltage. The indicator for the system is the computer readout of the voltage values using an Ardunio, calibrated to correspond to thickness values.

Unfortunately, a dowel used to hold the large gear and clamp together was not 3D printed, and thus a pen was used as a replacement. Although not ideal in terms of aesthetics, the system is still functional, nonetheless.

. Tron Days: Robotic Arm
Oct 2017

Previous Next

Tron Days is a two day event for first year Mechatronics Engineering students to allow hands-on experience with robotics (bonus: no lectures for two days!). The challenge was to design, build, and operate a tele-operated robotic manipulator. This robotic arm is used to pick up replacement bones and insert them into the patient.

The bones must be placed in the correct location and in the correct orientation. To minimize discomfort, the bone should be placed with as little adjustment as possible. The focus of the the tasks will be to place the femur and humerus in the correct locations.

The physical movement of the robot involved a pneumatic cylinder that lifted the wheels and turned them correspondingly, then placed it back down. The arm was controlled by motors to place the bones in their respective areas.
